Tsar Game Engine

The idea behind the engine

Having spent years programming with Unity, my team and I reached a consensus to develop our own game engine. Our decision was driven by the realization that Unity, while robust, falls short in optimization and suitability for larger titles. The proprietary nature of Unity also meant we were often left in the dark when the engine crashed, with no way to investigate the root causes.

Unity’s source code presents its own challenges, divided as it is between older, somewhat reliable code, and newer code that often feels glitchy and bug-ridden. A case in point is the VFX Graph; despite the underlying code being present, its binding is not externally exposed, limiting our ability to fully utilize it.

Why not opt for Unreal Engine? The answer is simplicity and efficiency. Unreal Engine, for all its capabilities, felt unnecessarily heavy for our purposes. We sought an engine that was both lightweight and potent. We experimented with Unreal Engine for about a month and a half but ultimately decided it didn’t meet our specific requirements, leading us to embark on the journey of creating a tailored solution that aligns with our vision and project needs.

Rendering?

The architectural journey of our game engine began with a deep dive into the world of graphics APIs, leading us to consider three main contenders: Vulkan, DirectX12, and OpenGL. Each API brought its unique advantages and challenges to the table.

OpenGL, despite its extensive user base and abundance of tutorials, was ultimately set aside. Its core architecture, rooted in an older state machine design, lacked the modern efficiency we sought. In contrast, Vulkan and DirectX12 were both engineered with multithreading support, a crucial feature for our purposes.

The decision between Vulkan and DirectX12 was not an easy one. It involved extensive research, reviewing numerous benchmarks, and evaluating the user base and available resources for each API. After careful consideration, we chose Vulkan. This decision was driven by Vulkan’s widespread device support, the wealth of quality learning materials, and its consistent and stable performance observed in various games. Furthermore, Vulkan’s API structure is notably more consistent and well-organized compared to its counterparts, aligning better with our vision for a high-performance, efficient rendering system.

Scripting?

At the outset of our engine development, we chose C# and Mono for scripting, largely due to our familiarity with these from our experience with Unity. A key aspect that made C# particularly appealing was its support for Reflection, a powerful feature for scripting. Reflection’s ability to handle implementations implicitly, without the need for explicit specifications, significantly streamlines the scripting process.

C# is not only user-friendly but also boasts speed and performance closely rivaling that of C++. However, after thorough consideration and analysis, we’ve recently decided to transition to C++. Despite C#’s efficiency and the availability of Profile Guided Optimizations (PGOs) that enhance its performance, C++ still outperforms in terms of speed. As a lower-level language compared to C#, C++ offers more direct control over system resources and hardware. This control, combined with C++’s inherent performance advantage, makes it a more suitable choice for our engine’s requirements.

In moving to C++, we plan to utilize ‘rttr’ library for reflection capabilities. Should rttr not meet our specific needs, we are prepared to develop our own reflection library to ensure our engine remains as efficient and adaptable as possible.
